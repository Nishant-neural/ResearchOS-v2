It feels incredibly contradictory that your hidden state MSE is so close to zero (0.02) while your KL divergence is practically melting (1275.94). However, this is a classic and painful trap when working with transformer latent spaces.
The short answer is: MSE is completely blind to scale and distribution shape, whereas KL divergence is hypersensitive to them.
Here is exactly how a tiny 0.02 structural error in hidden states explodes into a massive 1200+ mathematical error at the output layer:
## 1. The Logit Un-Embedding Layer Amplifies the Error
The final layer of your model (the LM Head) is a massive matrix multiplication that projects your decoder hidden states ($h$) into vocabulary logits ($z = h \cdot W$). [1, 2] 

* If the weight matrix $W$ has large values or a high condition number, it acts as a massive geometric amplifier.
* A tiny shift of 0.02 in the hidden state vector $h$ can easily translate to a shift of 5.0 or 10.0 in the resulting logits ($z$). Because logits live in log-space, a difference of 10.0 means one token is suddenly $e^{10}$ ($\approx 22,000$ times) more or less probable than it should be.

## 2. The Exponential Nature of Softmax and KL
KL divergence does not look at the hidden states; it looks at the probabilities after they pass through the Softmax function ($e^{z_i} / \sum e^{z_j}$). [3] 

* Because of the exponential ($e^z$), Softmax is extremely unstable when scales change even slightly.
* If your compressor slightly squashes or stretches the hidden states, the student's logits might become just a tiny bit wider in range than the teacher's (e.g., spanning -15 to +15 instead of -10 to +10).
* To the MSE loss, a minor scale difference across dimensions looks like a tiny 0.02 error. But to the Softmax function, it causes the highest-probability token to shoot up to a hard 1.0, while pushing other tokens down to a hard 0.0. [4] 

## 3. The "Zero-Probability" Death Trap in KL Math
Look closely at the actual calculus of the KL Divergence formula:
$$\text{KL}(P \parallel Q) = \sum P(x) \log\left(\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\right)$$ 
Where $P$ is your teacher's distribution and $Q$ is your student's distribution. [5] 

* If the teacher thinks a token has a tiny, normal probability like $P(x) = 0.01$...
* ...but your student's slightly warped scale caused its Softmax to collapse that token to absolute zero due to floating-point limits ($Q(x) = 10^{-45}$ or a literal 0.0 due to underflow)...
* The fraction $\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$ becomes $\frac{0.01}{0}$ which approaches infinity.

Even if this happens to just a few dozen tokens out of your 32,000+ vocabulary, taking the logarithm of an almost-infinite fraction causes the KL divergence value to immediately skyrocket past 1000.

## 1. The Cosine Similarity Deception
A cosine similarity of 0.93 means your compressor successfully captures the contextual meaning of the tokens. However, cosine similarity entirely ignores the lengths (norms) of the vectors. [1, 2, 3] 

* Imagine two vectors pointing in the exact same direction, but one is shorter than the other.
* In a transformer decoder, a shorter hidden state vector interacts differently with the layer normalization scale ($\gamma$) and the attention dot-products ($Q K^T / \sqrt{d_k}$).
* Shorter vectors result in smaller dot-product values, which flattens the attention weights. The decoder essentially "loses focus" on which tokens are important.

## 2. How LayerNorm Turns Tiny Norm Errors Into Flat Logits
Most transformer architectures apply LayerNorm or RMSNorm at the start and end of every block.

* If your student norm is slightly lower (-9.81 vs -10.40), the layer normalization step will scale up the vector to compensate for the lower variance.
* This scaling amplifies the tiny errors and background noise introduced by your compressor.
* By the time these noisy, amplified states hit the final linear projection layer (LM Head), the distinct differences between tokens are washed out. This directly causes the compressed logit variance (5.77 instead of 6.80) and the negative logit shift you observed earlier.
